If you have already used all of your vacation time or youre in a position that doesnt offer paid
time off, you may be able to take unpaid leave. Review your agreement with your client.
Your client may also have specific policies regarding different types of time off. For example,
you may be allotted three days for bereavement leave without it counting against your PTO.
## **Common types of time off**
- Bereavement
- Vacation
- Health or disability (illness, surgery, etc.)
- Personal time
- Maternity or paternity
- Leave of absence (short- or long-term)
## **Look at your clients calendar**
If your request for time off is flexible, check your client's calendar to see if the days you want to
take off conflict with their schedule of events. Consider requesting time off when you have
fewer tasks or projects to complete. If you're unsure, ask your employer what the best time is.
## **Discuss your request with your client**
Schedule a convenient time to discuss your request with your client. To increase your chances
of approval, suggest meeting after completing your daily tasks or finishing a large project. If
your client usually has extra time in the mornings, suggest meeting then.
It's usually not mandatory to provide a reason for a PTO request, unless you're planning an
extended absence. However, your client may appreciate knowing your general plans; for
example, mention that you're planning to attend a family reunion.
## **Send a formal request**
After speaking with your client, you may need to submit a formal request for time off. Some
clients may require a written email, while others may accept a WhatsApp message exchange.
Confirm how far in advance you need to make your request. Some clients may ask for one or
two weeks' notice, while others may require at least a month. The notice period may depend
on the length of time you plan to ask for. In an emergency, inform your client as soon as
possible.
For example, if you want to take one day off, your client may only need a week's notice. For
longer vacation periods, extended notice may be required so your client can find someone to
cover your duties. Depending on your client's policies, you may need to formally request time
off through email.

# How to **Resolve Impossible Tasks**
<aside>
**Task:** To develop a solution or output for tasks that are impossible to perform.
**Purpose:** The purpose is to identify solutions to tasks that may initially appear impossible
to complete. You should find ways and options for your client for each task assigned to you,
even if it is sometimes impossible to complete the task.
**End State:** You should be able to complete tasks that were previously believed to be
impossible by identifying and addressing the root causes of any obstacles. This may involve
finding alternative solutions or approaches to the problem.
</aside>
**Start of Process:** This task will start once an "impossible" task has been identified and steps
have been taken to break it down and find a solution.
---
<aside>
If you feel that a task is impossible, it is important to come prepared with a few potential
alternatives to present to the client. First, check for any schedule conflicts that may impact your
availability or the availability of other vendors. If you are unable to find any viable alternatives,
it is still important to communicate this to the client and show that you have conducted
thorough research in an effort to find a solution. Remember, it is okay if you are unable to find
a solution it is important to be honest, and transparent with your client.
</aside>
**Major Steps of the Process:**
1. **Define the task and clarify any ambiguities or uncertainties.**
- Take some time to understand exactly what the task is asking for and any specific
requirements or constraints.
- If there are any unclear or ambiguous parts, ask for clarification from your client or
someone who may have more information.
1. **Break the task down into smaller, more manageable parts.**
- If the task seems overwhelming or impossible, try breaking it down into smaller, more
manageable chunks.
- This can help make the task feel more doable and allow you to tackle it one step at a time.
1. **Identify any resources or support you may need.**
- Think about what resources or support you may need in order to complete the task.
- This could include things like additional information, assistance from others, or specialized
equipment or software.
1. **Develop a plan or strategy for completing the task.**
- Based on the information you have gathered and the resources available to you, come up
with a plan or strategy for how you will tackle the task.
- This may involve delegating certain parts of the task to others, seeking out additional
resources or support, or adjusting your approach based on what you have learned.
1. **Take action and make progress.**
- With your plan in place, start taking action to make progress on the task.
- Remember to stay focused and stay on track, and don't be afraid to ask for help or make
adjustments as needed.
1. **Review and assess your progress.**
- Periodically review your progress and assess whether your current approach is working or if
any adjustments need to be made.
- Be flexible and open to trying new approaches or seeking out additional resources if
needed.
